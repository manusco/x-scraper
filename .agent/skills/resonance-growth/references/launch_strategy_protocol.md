# Launch Strategy Protocol

> For planning product launches, feature announcements, and go-to-market execution.

## 1. The ORB Framework

Structure launch marketing across three channel types. Everything leads back to owned channels.

### Owned Channels
You control the channel (though not the audience). No algorithm or platform risk.

- Email list, blog, podcast, branded community (Slack/Discord), product itself
- Get more effective over time — direct relationship, compound value
- **Start with 1-2**: Lacks quality content → blog. Wants updates → email. Engagement matters → community.

### Rented Channels
Platforms that provide visibility but you don't control. Algorithms shift, pay-to-play increases.

- Social media (X, LinkedIn), app stores, YouTube, Reddit
- Pick 1-2 where your audience is active
- Use them to **drive traffic to owned channels**, not as primary strategy

### Borrowed Channels
Tap into someone else's audience to shortcut distribution.

- Guest content (blog posts, podcast interviews, newsletter features)
- Collaborations (webinars, co-marketing)
- Speaking engagements, influencer partnerships
- **Be proactive**: List industry leaders your audience follows → pitch win-win collaborations

**Key insight**: Borrowed channels give instant credibility. Rented channels give speed. Only owned channels give stability.

## 2. Five-Phase Launch

Launching isn't a one-day event. It's a phased process that builds momentum.

### Phase 1: Internal Launch
- Recruit early users one-on-one to test for free
- Collect feedback on usability gaps
- Goal: Validate core functionality with friendly users

### Phase 2: Alpha Launch
- Create landing page with early access signup
- Announce the product exists
- Invite users individually
- Goal: First external validation + initial waitlist

### Phase 3: Beta Launch
- Work through early access list (some free, some paid)
- Start marketing with teasers about problems you solve
- Recruit friends, investors, and influencers to test and share
- Consider: "Beta" badge, email invites, feature flags
- Goal: Build buzz + refine with broader feedback

### Phase 4: Early Access Launch
- Leak product details: screenshots, feature GIFs, demos
- Gather quantitative usage data
- Run product/market fit survey
- Throttle invites in batches (5-10% at a time) or invite all under "early access" framing
- Goal: Validate at scale + prepare messaging

### Phase 5: Full Launch
- Open self-serve signups
- Start charging (if not already)
- Announce general availability across all channels
- Touchpoints: customer emails, in-app popups, website banner, blog post, social posts, Product Hunt / BetaList / Hacker News
- Goal: Maximum visibility + conversion to paying users

## 3. Product Hunt Strategy

### Preparation
1. Build relationships with influential supporters and communities
2. Optimize listing: compelling tagline, polished visuals, short demo video
3. Study successful launches — identify what worked
4. Engage in relevant communities (provide value before pitching)
5. Prepare team for all-day engagement

### Launch Day
1. Treat it as an all-day event
2. Respond to every comment in real-time
3. Encourage existing audience to engage
4. Direct traffic back to your site for signups

### Post-Launch
1. Follow up with everyone who engaged
2. Convert PH traffic into owned relationships (email signups)
3. Continue momentum with post-launch content

## 4. Ongoing Launch Strategy

Don't rely on a single launch event. Every update is a launch moment.

| Update Size | Marketing Level | Channels |
|-------------|----------------|----------|
| Major (new features, product overhauls) | Full campaign | Blog, email, in-app, social, PR |
| Medium (integrations, UI enhancements) | Targeted announcement | Email to relevant segments, in-app banner |
| Minor (bug fixes, small tweaks) | Changelog only | Signal active development |

**Rules:**
- Space out releases — stagger announcements for sustained momentum
- Reuse high-performing tactics from previous launches
- Even small changelog updates remind customers you're building

## 6. Directory Distribution Strategy

Directories are an underrated launch channel. They provide backlinks, AI citation presence, and high-intent traffic.

### Why Directories Matter
1. **Backlink foundation**: Directory listings build domain authority for new sites.
2. **AI citation readiness**: AI answer engines crawl directories to discover products. Being listed = being citable.
3. **High-intent traffic**: Users browsing "[Category] tools" directories are actively evaluating.

### Directory Submission Playbook

**Phase 1: Foundation (Week 1)**
- Submit to major aggregators: Product Hunt, G2, Capterra, AlternativeTo, GetApp.
- Claim your listing on review sites: TrustPilot, Trustradius.
- Ensure NAP consistency (Name, Address, Phone) across all listings.

**Phase 2: Category-Specific (Week 2-3)**
- Identify niche directories for your industry.
- Submit to "awesome-*" GitHub lists (high DA backlinks).
- Find "best [category] tools" roundup posts → pitch the author to include you.

**Phase 3: Optimization (Ongoing)**
- Optimize each listing: unique description (not copy-pasted), screenshots, accurate categorization.
- Encourage reviews on high-authority platforms (G2, Capterra) post-activation.
- Monitor for broken or outdated listings quarterly.

### Destination Page Design
Every directory listing links somewhere. That page must convert.
- **Don't link to homepage** (too generic for the directory visitor's intent).
- **Create a tailored landing page**: "/from/[directory-name]" with messaging matching the directory's audience.
- **Include**: Clear value prop, CTA, social proof from similar users.
