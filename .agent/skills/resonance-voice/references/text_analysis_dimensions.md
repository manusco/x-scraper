# Text Analysis Dimensions — The Sherlock Toolkit

## Purpose

This is the analytical lens used during Phase A (Corpus Intake). For each piece of writing the subject provides, scan it against these 12 dimensions. Every observation you surface must:

1. Be **falsifiable** — the subject can confirm or deny it.
2. Have **a quote from the text** as evidence.
3. Be phrased as a **behavioral statement**, not an adjective.

> ❌ Low-signal: "You write with energy."
> ✅ High-signal: "You open with a declarative statement, never a question. Evidence: [quote]."

**The 12 dimensions apply to all subject types (Person, Brand, Role/Character).** What changes is how you interpret them. Type-specific interpretation notes appear under each dimension where behavior meaningfully differs.

---

## The 12 Dimensions

### 1. Sentence Length Distribution
**What to look for**: Is there a dominant rhythm? Short and punchy throughout? Long and rolling? Deliberate alternation?

- High-signal: "Sentences average 8–10 words, except when making a climactic point — those drop to 3–5. Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You write short sentences."

**Person**: Look for when the rhythm shifts — usually signals emotional temperature change.
**Brand**: Consistent sentence length often signals a deliberate style guide rule. Inconsistency signals no rule exists.
**Role/Character**: Consistent length is a character fingerprint — breaking it breaks character.

---

### 2. Paragraph Rhythm
**What to look for**: Single-sentence paragraphs as emphasis tools? Long dense blocks? A mix with a logic to it?

- High-signal: "Single-sentence paragraphs appear at transition points and after key claims — never mid-argument. Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You use short paragraphs."

**Brand**: Note whether short paragraphs are used to mimic conversational tone or to force scanability — different intentions, different rules.

---

### 3. Opening Moves
**What to look for**: How does every piece start? Statement of fact? A question? An image? A tension? A number? Throat-clearing or direct entry?

- High-signal: "Every piece opens with a declarative statement containing a tension or surprise. No questions. No 'Welcome to...' Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You have strong openings."

**Brand**: Check for consistency across channels. A brand that opens with questions on social but statements on web has two voices — note it.
**Role/Character**: Opening move is often the clearest character signal — how the character enters a conversation.

---

### 4. Closing Moves
**What to look for**: Hard conclusion? Call to action? Restatement? Trailing off? Does the close feel earned or appended?

- High-signal: "You close by returning to the opening claim — the last line echoes the first. Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "Your endings feel conclusive."

**Brand**: Brand closings often default to CTAs. Note whether the CTA is integrated into the voice or bolted on.

---

### 5. Punctuation Habits
**What to look for**: Em-dashes, ellipses, semicolons, parentheses, colons. Each carries a voice signature. Absence is also data.

- High-signal: "Em-dashes appear 3–5 times per piece, always mid-sentence for a sharp aside. Ellipses are absent. Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You use punctuation expressively."

**Brand**: Brands with strict style guides often have explicit punctuation rules. Inconsistency reveals the style guide is not followed or doesn't exist.
**Role/Character**: Punctuation habits are character-consistency signals — an AI character using ellipses when its profile bans them breaks character.

---

### 6. Vocabulary Density
**What to look for**: Precise technical words or concrete and plain? Field-specific jargon? Does the writer define terms or assume knowledge?

- High-signal: "Vocabulary is precise but non-academic. Specific nouns and active verbs. No jargon without context. Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You write clearly."

**Brand**: Does the brand own specific vocabulary? Words they use that competitors don't? Terminology they consistently avoid? Both are voice signals.
**Role/Character**: A character's vocabulary range is a core consistency rule — a working-class character using academic vocabulary is a character break.

---

### 7. Pronoun Stance
**What to look for**: I-heavy (personal, memoir)? You-heavy (reader-facing, instructional)? We (inclusive or corporate)? They (analytical, removed)?

- High-signal: "You-heavy in instructional sections, I-heavy when making a personal claim. 'We' only when including the reader in a shared belief. Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You use 'you' a lot."

**Person**: Pronoun shifts often signal shifts in emotional temperature. Track which pronoun appears at which moments.
**Brand**: "We" is the default corporate stance. Note whether it's used as genuine inclusion or distancing. Does the brand ever use "I"? If so, when?
**Role/Character**: A character's pronoun stance is a consistency rule. A first-person character who slips into third-person distance breaks the persona.

---

### 8. Structural Moves
**What to look for**: Headers, bullets, numbered lists — or flowing prose only? Do lists feel like a tool or a crutch? Mixed structures?

- High-signal: "No bullets or headers. Ideas are delivered as prose even when listing items — serialized with commas or 'and.' Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You prefer prose over lists."

**Brand**: Many brands have explicit formatting rules in style guides. The corpus reveals whether those rules are actually followed.
**Role/Character**: Characters in conversation never use bullets. If a character persona uses formatted lists in dialogue, that's a character break.

---

### 9. Recurring Metaphors or Frames
**What to look for**: Do the same comparisons appear across pieces? Physical, spatial, mechanical, biological, culinary, sports analogies?

- High-signal: "Three separate pieces use spatial metaphors ('room,' 'container,' 'edge'). Abstract ideas consistently framed in physical terms. Evidence: [quotes across pieces]."
- Low-signal: "You use metaphors well."

**Brand**: Brand metaphor families often reveal core positioning — a SaaS brand that consistently uses "infrastructure" metaphors is positioning itself differently from one that uses "relationship" metaphors.
**Role/Character**: Recurring metaphor families are part of character voice. Breaking the metaphor family breaks character.

---

### 10. Emphasis Signals
**What to look for**: Bold, italics, ALL CAPS, sentence isolation, repetition — how does the subject signal "this is the important part"?

- High-signal: "Emphasis is structural, never typographic. No bold or italics. A point gets emphasis by being given its own paragraph. Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You have a clear voice."

**Brand**: Brands often overuse bold or italics in marketing copy. Note whether emphasis is deliberate or reflexive.

---

### 11. Humor Register
**What to look for**: Is humor present at all? If yes: dry, self-deprecating, absurdist, observational? Does it appear at specific moments?

- High-signal: "Humor appears once per piece, as a short self-deprecating aside in the middle. Never at the opening, never at the close. Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You have a sense of humor."

**Person**: Note where humor appears — it often signals the subject's comfort level at that moment.
**Brand**: Humor in brand voice is high-risk / high-signal. Note whether it's earned or performed. A brand that uses humor in social but never in email has a context-dependent humor rule.
**Role/Character**: Humor register is a core character spec. A character who is "dry and deadpan" but occasionally cracks self-deprecating jokes needs both rules preserved.

---

### 12. Emotional Temperature
**What to look for**: Warm and confessional? Cool and analytical? Urgent and pressurized? Does temperature shift mid-piece or by topic?

- High-signal: "Default temperature is cool and observational. When making a personal claim, temperature rises — sentences shorten, pronouns shift to 'I.' Evidence: [quote]."
- Low-signal: "You write with emotional range."

**Brand**: Brand temperature is often misrepresented in guidelines ("we're warm and human") but not enacted in actual content. The corpus reveals the real temperature.
**Role/Character**: A character's emotional temperature is a consistency rule. Note what triggers temperature shifts — these are character moments.

---

## Observation Report Format

After analyzing the corpus, present each observation as:

```
[Dimension]: [Behavioral statement]. Evidence: "[quote from their text]." Is this deliberate?
```

Group observations. Lead with the strongest (most distinctive, most actionable for AI output). Cut anything generic — if it could describe 40% of writers or brands, it's not useful.

**Target**: 8–12 observations. Fewer means the corpus is too thin — request one more piece.
